               This work advances our understanding of the speed-accuracy trade-off (SAT) in human pointing movements, moving
         7
11             beyond the classical Fitts’ law, which models movement time (MT) as a function of target distance and size but
         8
12       9     overlooks individual strategy and variability in movement times. We propose a statistical framework that incorporates
13       10    user strategy and probabilistic descriptions of MT distributions. Using data from three experimental protocols and
14       11    four empirical studies, we analyze the influence of strategy on SAT. We model joint distributions of speed and accuracy
15       12
               with copulas, a novel statistical tool in HCI. Our contributions include a theoretical analysis of correlation as a
16       13
               measure of goodness of fit for MT models, parametric models linking MT to difficulty and strategy, and evaluation of
17       14
               data generation methods for realistic pointing simulations. This work offers a richer, distribution-based perspective on
18       15
               pointing performance. Code and parameters are provided for reproducibility and future research.

           First, Guiard and Rioul [39] make a strong case for considering strategy. In short, their argument is that a
26

     77
28   78    and changes in the contributions of 𝑥 and 𝑦 (e.g., if 𝑥 increases, 𝑦 decreases to keep the product 𝑥 × 𝑦 = 𝑘
29   79    constant). While Fitts’ law addresses conservation, it does not address how users allocate their effort towards
                                                          rR

32   82

37   88        Moving beyond Fitts’ law and its deterministic view of the SAT that focuses on means implies that we
38   89    adopt a statistical approach with a probabilistic interpretation, focused on distributions. Instead of describing
39   90
           the relationship between speed and accuracy in absolute terms (if one increases, the other decreases), we use
40   91

41   92

42   93

     95
44   96

7    367

8    368   One objective of this work is to generate joint distributions of ID𝑒 and MT, rather than some linear relation

15   376             the same function at the mean of those points; the two coincide only if the function is linear. This
16   377             point has been made before in the context of throughput [57].9 In HCI research, simulations are
17   378

                      COPULAS
35       450   A (bivariate) joint distribution describes the probability of two variables simultaneously; a marginal
36
                                                                                               iew

         451
               distribution represents the probability of a single variable when the other variable(s) are averaged out.
37       452
38             (Bivariate) copulas are a statistical function that uniquely describe the dependence between two random
         453
39       454   variables: any joint (bivariate) distribution can be expressed in terms of its marginal distributions and a
40       455   copula — a result known as Sklar’s theorem [55]. Thus, copulas “couple” [55, Chapter 1] two marginals
41       456
               into a joint distribution; they thus capture how variables depend on each other irrespective of their actual
42       457
               values. Copulas are parametric and describe how the two variables are associated, for example imposing a
43       458
               prescribed (rank) correlation, everywhere, or only at their tails.
         459
44       460        To illustrate the usefulness of copulas, consider a model for generating synthetic human height and
45       461
46             weight data. Both height and weight are likely to follow a Gaussian distribution, and with access to a basic
         462
47       463
               anthropomorphic dataset, we can estimate their parameters. A simple, yet flawed, approach to generating
48       464   realistic human data would be to treat height and weight as independent variables: first sampling height,
49       465   then independently sampling weight. This approach, however, ignores the natural correlation between height
50       466

6    1718
            9.1    Copula fits
7    1719

8    1720   We used copulas to characterize the dependency between ID𝑒 and MT. This work suggests there is very
9    1721
            little local dependency between ID𝑒 and MT i.e., for a given (D,W) pair: ID𝑒 and MT be considered close to
10   1722
            independent. When it comes to strategy, the local dependencies are marginally stronger. We interpret this as
11   1723
     1724   meaning that within a (D,W) condition, movements are essentially equivalent: due to variability, people may
12
     1725   be both more precise and faster, and the speed-accuracy tradeoff does not necessarily apply. This is less
13   1726
14   1727
            true within a given strategy: one hypothesis is that participants do not ”aim” for the same strategy within
15   1728
            blocks. In particular, what is likely is that they aim relatively to the previous block i.e., if the last block was
16   1729   a speed condition, the participant will aim to be a bit slower than before if the new block is a balanced
17   1730   condition. Hence, data within the same strategy condition may actually be performed with different intent
18   1731
            by the participants.
19   1732

                                 Fo
20   1733

21   1734

            12.2    On the local and global dependency
37   2272
38   2273   In this work, we investigated both local and global dependencies between ID𝑒 and MT, representing two
39   2274
            forms of the speed-accuracy tradeoff (SAT): a local SAT and a global SAT. Our observation of minimal to no
40   2275
            local dependency suggests two possible explanations: either the variability in movement time is substantially
41   2276
            larger than that of ID𝑒 , or, from a motor control perspective, movements performed under a given condition
42   2277
            are functionally equivalent. The concept of SAT operating at different scales is well-established in the study of
43   2278
     2279
44   2280
            reaction time (RT) within psychophysical tasks, commonly referred to as the micro and macro SATs [14, 18].
45   2281
            A proposed explanation for the micro SAT is that individuals continuously adjust their speed-accuracy
46   2282   tradeoffs while responding [18]. Although it is uncertain whether findings from RT in psychophysical tasks
47   2283   directly translate to movement tasks, our results align with this broader understanding: the relationship
48   2284
            between MT and ID𝑒 is stronger in tasks involving strategic decision-making, where participants are explicitly
49   2285
            encouraged to manage the speed-accuracy tradeoff.

     2452
12          The SAT is well known in HCI: in most tasks, to achieve greater precision, users generally need to reduce
     2453
13   2454   their speed. However, reliably manipulating speed and accuracy conditions in controlled experiments is
14   2455   challenging; in pointing, at least four distinct experimental protocols have been proposed.
15   2456
16   2457   A.1    Fitts’ protocol
17   2458
18   2459   The most widely used protocol is Fitts’, where accuracy is dictated by the width of the target: the independent
19   2460   variables are target distance (D) and width (W), while movement time (MT) serves as the dependent
                                 Fo
20   2461
            variable. This paradigm closely aligns with meaningful HCI tasks, like icon selection, since target geometry
21   2462
            is explicitly part of the task. However, participants often fail to fully adhere to target width constraints,
22

     2464   either overusing or underusing the available target space [37, 71].

                                                    ee

     2468

28   2470   to rapid movement studies, which are of lesser importance to HCI, and we do not consider this protocol
29   2471
            further.

     Traditional Fitts’ law captures average trends in pointing tasks but ignores variability, strategy effects, and complex dependencies
7
8
     between movement time (MT) and effective index of difficulty (ID𝑒 ). In this work, we adopt a probabilistic perspective to model the full
9    distribution of MTs and ID𝑒 , and how this relationship depends on user strategy. We analyze data from four empirical studies using
10   three different experimental protocols that manipulate task constraints and user strategy across different input modalities, examining
11   how speed and accuracy are traded off locally (within conditions) and globally (across conditions). We find strong global dependencies
12   but minimal local dependencies, suggesting movements within a condition are largely functionally equivalent. To capture behavioral
13
     distributions, we introduce a probabilistic ID𝑒 model, a bivariate Gaussian model linking ID𝑒 and mean movement time with strategy,
14
     as well as three models based on copulas and Exponentially Modified Gaussian (EMG) noise distributions that express the complete
15
16
     joint (ID𝑒 , MT) distribution, all of which are parametrized by user strategy. These three models are compared on their ability to
17   faithfully reproduce summary statistics of the pointing datasets. This work offers a richer, distribution-and-strategy-based perspective
18   on pointing performance. Code and parameters are provided for reproducibility and future research.

     1   INTRODUCTION
27
28   Pointing tasks, such as selecting an icon with a mouse, are among the most fundamental interactions in human-
29   computer interaction (HCI) [7, 59]. The dominant quantitative model of pointing, Fitts’ law [21, 30, 59], predicts the
30
     mean movement time (MT) required to reach a target of width 𝑊 at distance 𝐷:
31
                                                                                      𝐷
32                                                               MT = 𝑎 + 𝑏 log2 (1 + 𝑊 ),                                                                 (1)
33
34                                                                     ≡ 𝑎 + 𝑏 ID.                                                                         (2)
35
36
     Here, ID = log2 (1 + 𝐷/𝑊 ) is the index of difficulty, and the parameters 𝑎 and 𝑏 are empirically estimated. Fitts’ law
37   captures the speed-accuracy trade-off (SAT) in motor control: smaller targets, which require more precision, take
38   longer to acquire. It has been validated across devices, modalities, and populations, and remains a cornerstone of input
39
     evaluation and interface design [7, 59].
40
41
         Yet, Fitts’ law provides only a partial account of the SAT. It predicts average performance but ignores trial-to-
42   trial variability. In reality, MTs are not fixed values but they are spread across a distribution, and some attempts are
43   unexpectedly fast and precise while others are slow and inaccurate.

53       Fitts’ law also leaves no room for user strategy, where participants may favor speed or accuracy depending on
54
      instructions or personal preference [38, 71], which conflates intentional and unintentional variability, and as we will
55
      show, variability in MT and user strategy strongly influence observed responses: ignoring them oversimplifies pointing
56
57    behavior and limits the predictive power of models. To address these limitations, we adopt a probabilistic perspective
58    that treats both movement time and accuracy as random variables drawn from a joint distribution, conditioned on task
59
      and strategy. Rather than assuming that going faster always means being less accurate, or that smaller targets always
60
      lead to lower MTs, we examine how user strategies and task properties influence the relationship between the observed
61
62    MTs and accuracy.
63       We examine the dependence between movement time and accuracy at two levels of analysis. At the local level, we
64
      study the relationship between MT and accuracy within individual task conditions (given 𝐷, 𝑊 , and strategy). At the
65
      global level, we pool data across conditions. This distinction is important, as we find that the SAT is often weak or
66
67    absent when examined locally, but emerges clearly at the global level. Understanding this contrast helps explain when
68    trade-offs appear in experimental data and how they should be modeled.
69
         To model this dependence, we rely on the exponentially modified Gaussian (EMG) distribution, which is parametric
70
      model that was previously validated to model MT distributions [24, 27, 43, 73], but without accounting for strategy.
71
72    The EMG model describes MT conditional on accuracy with a linear mean and a quadratic variance, thus imposing a
73    specific dependency structure. While the EMG model captures important aspects of the SAT, it imposes the form of
74
      dependence a priori. Thus, to allow for more flexibility, we also consider models based on copulas, which describe the
75
      joint distribution of MT and accuracy without assuming a fixed pattern of dependence. Together, these approaches let
76
77    us compare descriptions of the SAT across experimental conditions.
78       To ground these perspectives, we analyze pointing data collected under three experimental protocols that differ
79
      in how accuracy is defined: (1) the classic Fitts protocol, where accuracy is set by target width 𝑊 ; (2) a pure strategy
80
      protocol, where accuracy is determined by participants in line with experimenter instructions; and (3) a combined
81
82    Fitts-with-strategy protocol that merges both task and strategy constraints. Together, these protocols reveal how task
83    properties and strategy interact to shape pointing performance.
84

93

      a richer account of the SAT in pointing and introduces tools that better reflect the variability and strategies observed in
102
      real interaction.

105   2     RELATED WORK
106
107
      Pointing is an instance of a goal-directed movement, which has been extensively described in the motor control
108   literature (see e.g., [61, Section 2] or [18] for an introduction). The SAT in pointing has been known at least since
109   Woordworth [67], and is classically captured by Fitts’ law for goal directed movements, and Schmidt’s law [57] for fast,
110
      open loop movements. In HCI, pointing is one of the fundamental elementary interactions of users with GUI; the topic
111
112
      has, and continues to attract attention.
113
114   2.1    Fitts’ law extensions
115
      Many studies have extended Fitts’ law, and namely its applicability to novel paradigms such as 2D and 3D pointing, by
116
117   proposing a novel index of difficulty. For instance, Accot and Zhai [2] review several indices for bivariate pointing, with
118   additional insights provided by [33], while Murata and Iwase [49] extend the framework to trivariate pointing. These
119   models however do not provide MT distributions and overlook the impact of user strategy on performance. Another
120
      line of research focuses on modeling the spatial distribution of movement endpoints. Notable examples include the
121
122   sum-of-Gaussians models [6, 69, 72]. While these models capture endpoint distributions, their treatment of movement
123   time remains based on averages. Additionally, no sum-of-Gaussians model incorporates parameters that directly account
124   for user strategy to our knowledge.
125
126
      2.2    Pointing with different strategies
127
128   Most people will regularly over or under utilize the target width W [59], creating, according to Zhai et al. an additional
129
      subjective layer of speed-accuracy tradeoff [71]. This strategic choice may be affected by context, for example people
130
      will favor accuracy in when safety matters critically [37]. Various works have thus investigated the effect of strategy on
131
132   pointing performance. This includes work on the invariance of throughput for different user strategies [4, 46, 54] and
133   the comparison of evaluations of different interaction techniques [68], but has also lead researchers to define variations
134
      on Fitts’ protocols, further described Appendix A. Here we focus on existing models that account explicitly for strategy.
135

      2.3    Pointing models that describe the distribution of MT
174   2.3.1 Fitts’ law, regression, and the implicit noise model . The expression for Fitts’ law Equation 3 masks an underlying
175
      statistical model that assumes additive, centered noise3 :
176
177                                         MT = 𝑎 + 𝑏ID𝑒 + noise (additive),               E[noise] = 0 (centered).                                          (5)
178
179   Pearson’s 𝑟 is commonly used as a goodness-of-fit measure. However, as shown by Drewes [17] and Gori et al. [32],
180
      the Pearson correlation between MT and ID𝑒 , 𝑟 (MT, ID𝑒 ), often yields moderate to low values in practice on pointing
181
      datasets, with some cases showing correlations close to zero [30].
182
183      In fact, to mitigate variability in MT and increase the goodness-of-fit measure, researchers typically compute the
184   sample average of MT, denoted in the remainder of the work as MT4 , before calculating 𝑟 . Consequently, the model
185
      actually considered by many researchers is:
186
187                                         MT = 𝑎 + 𝑏ID𝑒 + noise (additive),               E[noise] = 0 (centered),                                          (6)
188
189   with the goodness-of-fit measure being 𝑟 (MT, ID𝑒 ). Fitts’ law is thus essentially a Gaussian model for average pointing
190   time MT.
191
192   2.3.2 Asymmetric MT distributions and the Exponentially Modified Gaussian (EMG) noise model . Rather than averaging
193
      out the noise as in Equation 6 and describing only MT, other works model the entire noise distribution instead, and
194
195
      show that the conditional distribution of MT for a given ID or ID𝑒 level is distributed according to an asymmetric,
196   positive (right) skewed [9, 24, 27, 41, 43, 53, 73] distribution. While several distributions have been suggested, including
197   gamma, Gumbel and lognormal, we follow Gori and colleagues [24, 27, 43], and Zhao et al. [73] who considered an
198
      Exponentially Modified Gaussian (EMG) noise model: it is positive, is assymetric, has parameters that are easy to
199
200
      interpret and has shown good performance, including when modeling steering data [64].
201      Gori [24] further showed that a quadratic variance model, where the standard deviation of the MT distribution
202   increases linearly with its mean, provided a good fit to empirical data, which was later confirmed by Zhao et al. [73]
203
      who similarly found that quadratic variance models provided the best fit among the models they tested.

225   2.4    Models that simulate entire trajectories
226
      2.4.1 Optimal feedback control models. Several optimal feedback control (OFC) models for pointing have been applied
227
228
      in HCI [20, 42], following from the seminal work by Todorov and Jordan [60]. These predict complete movement
229   trajectories as well as their spatial variability. Unfortunately, these models are not well suited to predict movement time
230   and its associated variability, since OFC doesn’t have an intrinsic mechanism to stop controlling, and thus it requires
231
      defining a stoping condition (see e.g., [56, Section 3.4]). 5 Solutions to this problem include integrating an explicit cost
232
233
      for time, as in [5], but to our knowledge there is currently no OFC model that has predicted realistic MT distributions.
234   Reinforcement learning approaches, which can be viewed as methods to solve optimal control problems when the
235   dynamics of the controled system are unknown, share the same limitation: although they can learn policies that lead
236
      to realistic movement trajectories and can produce data compatible with Fitts’ law [10, 40], they similarly require
237
238
      stopping criteria or cost of time [20], and to our knowledge, have never been shown to generate realistic distributions
239   of movement times.
240
241   2.4.2 Simulation models. Optimal control models typically have closed-form solutions or can be computed numerically
242
      with sufficient speed to enable real-time implementation. In contrast, more complex approaches, to which we refer
243
      as simulation models, have also been proposed. For instance, Do et al. [15] present an intermittent control model
244
245   for pointing that incorporates various sources of variability, including motor and perceptual noise. However, this
246   model does not report movement time (MT) variability, and the role of user strategy in its framework remains unclear.
247
      Additionally, the model required six days of training. Similarly, Moon et al. [47] introduce a movement model trained
248
      using proximal policy optimization, a deep reinforcement learning technique, but the study does not report on MT
249
250   variability and required approximately 40 hours of training. Thus, while simulation models can be used to generate
251   movement time trial data, to our knowledge, no such results have been reported, nor on the variability of MT. Moreover,
252
      while it should be possible, it remains unclear at this point how these models could be adapted to account for user
253
      strategy. Finally, their lengthy training times and the efforts required for their implementation undermine their utility
254
255   as simple, parametric models, which are the focus of this work.

      3.1    Theoretical analysis of the 𝑟 2 (MT, ID𝑒 ) correlation
264   The goodness of fit of Fitts’ law is usually justified via Pearson correlation 𝑟 2 (MT, ID𝑒 ), which is close to 1 in many
265
      experiments where accuracy is manipulated by W [59]. However, we show that many different MT distributions can
266
267
      achieve 𝑟 2 (MT, ID𝑒 ) close to 1. Thus, using alignement with Fitts’ law is a weak criterion to assess the fit of a model for
268   distribution of movement times.
269
270       Proposition 3.1. Any conditional distribution 𝑝 (MT|ID𝑒 ) will reach 𝑟 2 (MT, ID𝑒 ) = 1 if it has a conditional expectation
271   E[MT|ID𝑒 ] that is linear in ID𝑒 .
272
273       The proof is given in Appendix B, and relies on the fact that Pearson correlation is a function of the covariance,
274   which measures linear deviations from the mean. Several distributions that have been previously used for modeling
275
      pointing times satisfy this condition
276
277          • The EMG model with quadratic variance [24, 27, 43, 73] (Equation 8) has linear conditional mean.
278          • A gamma [41] distribution with shape 𝑘 and scale 𝜃 has mean value 𝑘𝜃 . Having 𝑘 or 𝜃 increase linearly with
279
               ID𝑒 would fulfill the condition.
280
281          • A generalized extreme value [9] distribution GEV(𝜇, 𝜎, 𝑠) has a mean value that increases linearly with 𝜇. Having
282            𝜇 as a linear function of ID𝑒 would thus also fulfil the condition.
283
      There are many more distributions possible, since one can force a conditional linear mean into any continuous
284
285   distribution whose mean can be set freely. Two important takeaways follow: First, models of movement time must have
286   a conditional mean linear in ID𝑒 to satisfy Fitts’ law. Second, it motivates the need to turn to measures of dependences
287   that are more sophisticated than Pearson correlation.
288
          In practice, MT may refer to different aggregated quantities, depending on how data are collapsed across conditions,
289
290   participants, and repetitions [54]. For example: (i) averaging across all (D,W) pairs [59], (ii) averaging over ID levels
291   (noting that multiple (D,W) pairs can map to the same ID [32]), or (iii) aggregating across participants [1, 46]. When
292   blocks are repeated, aggregation may again be performed over repetitions [36, 41]. Each additional aggregation step
293
      tends to inflate 𝑟 2 (MT, ID𝑒 ) by the same mechanism outlined in the proof of the Proposition.
294
295
296   3.2    Measuring the dependence between two random variables with bivariate copulas
297
      In the previous section, we argued that Pearson correlation 𝑟 2 is a limited tool for evaluating Fitts’ law models because
298
      it only reflects linear associations between mean MT and ID𝑒 , and ignores other aspects of the joint distribution. To
299
300   move beyond this limitation, we need methods that capture dependence structures more generally.
301       Copulas are mathematical functions that describe the dependence structure between two random variables, indepen-
302
      dent of their individual distributions. Any bivariate joint distribution can be decomposed into its marginals, which
303
      describe the behavior of each variable separately, and a copula, which describes how the two variables are linked
304
305   (Sklar’s theorem [52]). Thus copulas “couple” [52, Chapter 1] two marginals, and capture the dependence between two
306   variables even when the actual scales or distributions of the variables have been factored out, thus going well beyond
307
      what linear correlation can reveal.
308
          To illustrate the usefulness of copulas, consider a model for generating synthetic human height and weight data.
309
310   Both height and weight are likely to follow a Gaussian distribution, and with access to a basic anthropomorphic dataset,
311   we can estimate their parameters. A simple, yet flawed, approach to generating realistic human data would be to treat
312                                                                  6
      Pointing Beyond Fitts: Strategy, Movement Time Distributions, Local-Global Tradeoffs, and Generative Models
                                                                                                           CHI ’24, May 11–16, 2024, Honolulu, HI, USA


313   height and weight as independent variables: first sampling height, then independently sampling weight. This approach,
314
      however, ignores the natural correlation between height and weight: taller individuals tend to weigh more than shorter
315
      individuals, and this relationship needs to be captured for a realistic model. Copulas not only allow us to imply a
316
317   dependence structure between weight and height; but different copulas specify different forms of dependencies between
318   weight and height.
319
         Figure 1 illustrates how the Gaussian copula shapes a joint distribution: In (d), the two marginals (a) and (b) are
320
      independently combined. In (e), the two marginals are coupled with a Gaussian copula (c). For this illustration, we
321
322   have used realistic marginal distributions for MT (a) and ID (b). Note that in both jointplots (d) and (e), the estimated
323   marginals are identical, but notice how the datasets differ. In particular, we see that the dataset obtained with the
324
      Gaussian copula has variance that increases with ID levels, which is characteristic of pointing data [24].
325
         Copulas are parametric and describe how the two variables are associated, for example imposing a prescribed
326
327   (rank) correlation, everywhere, or only at their tails. For example a Gumbel copula introduces so-called upper tail
328   dependence [52], where if one variable takes on an “extreme” high value, the other is also likely to take on an “extreme”
329
      high value. These copulas are used for variables that “reinforce” themselves e.g., when modeling extreme financial
330
      events [14]. Because each copula family captures different types of dependences (linear, non-linear, tail-heavy etc.) we fit
331
332   several candidates and retain the one that best approximates the observed data. Copulas are fitted with likelihood-based
333   procedures available in statistical libraries that support copulas. The way we identify copulas in this work is further
334
      described subsection C.1. All copulas used in this work are displayed for two sets of marginal distributions in Figure 18:
335
      standard normal marginals, which is a classical way of representing copulas, and marginals estimated from a pointing
336
337   dataset.

465
                geometry and strategy i.e., the remaining effect of s, D and W on MT are likely second order. The conditional

469            distribution of MT given ID𝑒 is modeled with the exponentially modified Gaussian (EMG) model described
470
               in subsubsection 2.3.2. We also model 𝑝 (ID𝑒 |𝐷,𝑊 , 𝑠) enabling the model to be used not just descriptively, but
471

          (2) Copula-based approach. The EMG approach fixes the dependency structure between MT and 𝐼𝐷𝑒 through
477
478            its quadratic variance model. To allow more general forms of dependence, and thus study the SAT from a
479            perspective that is agnostic to any form of dependence, we instead model the joint distribution using copulas,
480
               which capture the dependency between two variables independently of their marginals. This approach removes
481
               the restriction imposed by the EMG variance model and provides a more flexible description of MT and ID𝑒 .

      Learning the global dependency 𝑝 (MT, ID𝑒 ) proceeds analogously by dropping the conditioning on D, W, and 𝑠. We
484
485
      apply these two modeling approaches to three datasets collected under progressively richer experimental protocols,
486   moving from a classic Fitts’ law task and a strategy-based task to a combined Fitts-with-strategy task.
487
488      Study 1: Fitts protocol. In the first study (section 5), we re-analyze data of a standard Fitts protocol (subsection A.1),
489
      where accuracy is controlled by target width W. Because this dataset was previously used to validate the EMG
490
491
      distribution by two independent works [24, 73], we focus here on copulas. Several candidate copula models were
492   considered to describe the global and local dependencies; the rotated Gumbel copula was selected as the best fit for
493   the global distribution, capturing strong correlation in the lower tail. For the local distributions, a low-correlation
494
      Gaussian copula provided the best fit. A linear model relating ID𝑒 to ID is proposed to form the conditional distribution
495
496
      𝑝 (ID𝑒 |ID, D, W). This study shows that the speed-accuracy tradeoff emerges globally, not locally.
497
498      Study 2: Pure strategy protocol. In the second study (section 6), we analyze data from a pure strategy protocol
499   (subsection A.3), where accuracy is determined only by the participant’s strategy 𝑠. As a first step, we examine the joint
500
      distribution of MT and ID𝑒 , and propose a bivariate Gaussian model parametrized by 𝑠. This simplified, aggregated
501
502
      view, highlights systematic effects of strategy on speed and accuracy without the additional variability present in single
503   trials. This is important since unlike task parameters D and W, the influence of strategy alone on the speed-accuracy
504   trade-off is less well modeled. Thus, this preliminary analysis provides a new, simple model and provides a broad trend
505
      and some intuition for the more detailed analysis that follows. Next, we show that the EMG model with quadratic
506
507
      variance also fits this dataset, extending its applicability. Finally, we find that the Gaussian and t-copulas provide the
508   best fit for 𝑝 (MT, ID𝑒 |𝑠) and 𝑝 (MT, ID𝑒 ). This study shows that user strategy directly shapes the dependency structure,
509   highlighting the importance of modeling strategy explicitly.
510
511
         Study 3: Fitts-with-strategy protocol. In the third study (section 7), we combine both approaches to manipulate
512
513
      accuracy, by analyzing data from a Fitts-with-strategy protocol (subsection A.4), where accuracy is influenced by both
514   target width W and strategic instructions 𝑠. We model the local distribution 𝑝 (MT, ID𝑒 |𝑠, D, W), and confirm that the
515   EMG with quadratic variance and asymmetric distribution provides good fits. As in the previous study, Gaussian and
516
      t-copulas yield the best joint models. We also extend the linear model relating ID𝑒 to ID by including a term for strategy.
517
518
      This extension makes explicit how strategy interacts with task difficulty to shape user accuracy, as captured through
519   ID𝑒 .

524   We used an existing dataset by Jude et al. [41] (JGP). The JGP dataset was generated using a 2D multi-directional tapping
525

528   (px)) fully crossed, which makes 10 unique ID levels ranging from 1.52 to 4.58 bit. At 20 trials per block, the entire
529   dataset consists of 21600 movements. We noticed a bug in the dataset we downloaded, where the 2 last replications are
530
      identical to the two first, so we discarded the 2 last replications from the dataset, leaving us with 4 replications (14400
531
532

533
534   5.2    Fitting EMG on MT given ID𝑒 values
535
536   We fitted the EMG model for each participant and for each replication. In line with existing works on this dataset [24, 73],
537   the EMG fit was largely superior to the Gaussian fit obtained via linear regression. We namely found AIC differences
538
      routinely above 300; typically a difference of 10 is already considered strongly discriminative. A pair-plot of the fitted
539
      parameters can be found in the supplementary materials.

542   5.3    Fitting copulas on (MT, ID𝑒 )
543
544
      5.3.1 Local dependency structure. To test whether the SAT appears within individual task conditions, we examined the
545   local dependency between MT and ID𝑒 through 𝑝 (MT, ID𝑒 |D, W). If the SAT held locally, one would expect systematic
546   dependence between the two variables when D and W are fixed.
547
          We compared candidate copula models using the model evidence ratio R as explained in subsection C.2. This ratio
548
549
      expresses how likely one model is relative to the best-fitting model: for example, R = 0.1 means the model is about 10
550   times less likely to be the true model than the current best fitting. This allows us to compare copulas on a common scale.
551       Pooled over participants and across D×W pairs, the independent copula was most often selected as the best-fitting
552
      model (see Figure 3a). This indicates that within a given condition, longer movement times were not reliably associated
553
554
      with higher effective difficulty. In other words, the local dependency between MT and ID𝑒 was weak or absent.
555       An exception occurred for the smallest target condition (W = 64), where both Spearman’s 𝜌 and Kendall’s 𝜏
556   correlations were slightly negative. This suggests that in this extreme case, faster movements could even be associated
557
      with greater accuracy, the opposite of the expected SAT. While weak, this paradoxical result highlights that the large
558
559
      trial-to-trial variability can obscure or even invert the trade-off locally.
560       These findings suggest that the SAT is not strongly expressed at the local level, where performance appears largely
561   independent once task parameters are fixed.

658   5.5    Interpretation of the first study
659
      This study examined dependencies between ID𝑒 and MT under Fitts’ protocol, where accuracy is imposed by target
660
661   width W. At the local level, copula fits showed little to no correlation between ID𝑒 and MT within a given condition, with
662   the smallest target size even yielding a slight negative correlation. This suggests that, when accuracy is specified a priori,
663   movements within a fixed (D,W) pair are essentially equivalent with respect to the SAT: variability in execution reflects
664
      motor noise more than systematic planning trade-offs. The analysis at the global level revealed a clear dependence,
665
666   showing that the SAT only emerges across task conditions. The dependence was best captured by the rotated Gumbel
667   copula, which emphasizes lower-tail dependence. This implies a strong correlation between low values of ID𝑒 and MT
668   i.e., a small spread of the MT data for low values of ID𝑒 , which is consistent with the quadratic variance model used in
669
      the EMG model. Finally, we analyzed the relationship between ID𝑒 and ID. Possible D and W effects were weak, and
670
671   not always significant, but could have been misestimated due to collinearity between D, W and ID. Thus, to adress
672   these collinearity issues, we fit the linear models with ridge regression with cross-validation9 . Although detailed results
673
      9 Ridge regression is a technique for fitting linear models when collinearity exists among predictors, as it produces more stable and reliable estimates of
674
      effects [34]. The method introduces a regularization term controlled by a hyperparameter known as the ridge parameter. When the ridge parameter is set
675   to 0, ridge regression reduces to the traditional ordinary least squares approach. As the ridge parameter increases, the method progressively shrinks
676                                                                                13
      CHI ’24, May 11–16, 2024, Honolulu, HI, USA                                                                                                       Julien Gori


677   are not reported here, the estimated coefficient for ID was 0.94 i.e., closer to 1 than the estimates from the presented
678
      linear models. This suggests that the presented effects of D and W are actually slightly inflated by collinearity. Thus,
679
      this further supports that ID is the dominant predictor for ID𝑒 , and that additional effects of D and W are negligible.

      In the pure strategy protocol (see subsection A.3), accuracy is not manipulated by W but by the experimenter instructing

      6.1    The GO dataset

689   We investigate the effect of participant strategy on movement time and ID𝑒 , using the Guiard-Olafsdottir (GO) dataset [36].
690   Participants had to aim at a line (1D task), with five different strategies, from speed emphasis allowing a large spread of
691
      endpoints, to precision emphasis where the goal is to target the 1-pixel-wide line. For comparison, the instruction in a
692
693   standard Fitts experiment is usually between the speed and balanced condition, depending on the experimenter. The 16
694   participants performed 5 strategies, each replicated 5 times. We thus have 25 blocks per participant, each of about 15 to
695   20 movements each. We used a pre-processed version of the dataset used in [28]. One participant had partially corrupt
696
      data; we removed this participant entirely for safety. We also removed 79 outlier points, given the recording procedure
697
698   was sometimes unreliable. The resulting dataset is composed of 15 × 5 × 5 = 375 different experimental blocks for a
699   total of 5858 movements.

      6.5        Interpretation of the second study
925
926   This second study examined the dependence between ID𝑒 and MT within the pure strategy protocol, where accuracy is
927
      specified by instructions rather than target width. As in the first study, we found strong global dependence between
928
929
      ID𝑒 and MT when data is pooled across conditions (here, strategy), while within conditions, local dependence remains
930   weak.
931        We first confirmed that the data from the pure strategy protocol are compatible with Fitts’ law. When aggregated by
932
      strategy, ID𝑒 and MT were almost perfectly correlated (𝜌 = 0.97). To capture the effect of strategy, we introduced a
933
934
      bivariate Gaussian model parameterized by strategy. This model is both simple and interpretable: it reproduces Fitts’ law
935   12 A violin plot of the estimated Galambos copula parameters is provided in the Supplementary Materials.

936                                                                                                                    18
      Pointing Beyond Fitts: Strategy, Movement Time Distributions, Local-Global Tradeoffs, and Generative Models
                                                                                                           CHI ’24, May 11–16, 2024, Honolulu, HI, USA


937   globally, and shows positive within-strategy correlations between ID𝑒 and MT, except in the speed-emphasis condition.
938
      The latter exception aligns with the idea of ballistic, feedforward movements [11, 22], where accuracy no longer relates
939
      to movement time.
940
941       Concerning distributional modeling of MT, as expected, the EMG model outperformed simpler Gaussian alternatives,
942   thanks to its ability to capture both long-tailed distributions and variance that increases with difficulty. This replicates
943
      the findings from the Fitts protocol, suggesting that the properties making the EMG model effective are general
944
      characteristics of movement, and not protocol-specific.
945
946       Finally, we assessed dependence using copulas. At the local level, low correlation Gaussian copulas provided the best
947   fit in nearly all conditions, indicating a weak but symmetric form of dependence between MT and ID𝑒 . The balanced
948
      strategy was a slight exception, where the Galambos copula gave a marginally better fit, though with parameter values
949
      that made it a close match to the independent copula. At the global level, pooling across strategies revealed much
950
951   stronger dependencies: Gaussian and 𝑡-copulas dominated13 , with an average correlation of 𝜌 ≈ 0.71. This shows
952   that the speed-accuracy trade-off becomes most apparent once strategic variation is aggregated, consistent with the
953
      global-local contrast observed in the first study.

956   7     THIRD STUDY: DEPENDENCE BETWEEN ID𝑒 AND MT IN THE FULL PROTOCOL
957
      The third study builds directly on the first two by combining their respective manipulations of accuracy. Whereas
958
      the first study controlled accuracy through target width W and the second study controlled accuracy through explicit
959
960   strategy instructions, the YKORM dataset [68] used a Fitts-with-strategy protocol in which both W and strategy were
961   varied simultaneously (see subsection A.4). This allows us to test whether the patterns observed separately in the Fitts
962
      and pure strategy protocols also hold when both factors operate together.
963
964
965
      7.1    The YKORM dataset
966
      The original study’s objective was to compare two existing interaction techniques (bubble cursor and bayesian touch
967
      criterion) against regular pointing, across strategies. The study used an ISO circle-of-circles tasks with distractors. The
968
969   experiment fully crossed 4 factors: D (400, 770)px, W (8, 24, 70)px, strategy (fast, balanced, accurate) and a parameter for
970   the distractor density (3 levels), which provides an ideal design to examine how task geometry and strategy together
971
      shape the joint distribution of MT and ID𝑒 . For comparison with the two previous studies, we only used regular (mouse-
972
      based) pointing data, and aggregated data across distractor density levels. Twelve participants completed 23 trials per
973
974   condition, which makes 12 participants × 3 W × 2 D × 3 strategy × 69 movements = 216 sets × 69 movements = 14904
975   trials. The first 3 trial of each set were discarded (648) with some outliers (244), leaving us with 14012 data points.
976
977
      7.2    Conformity with Fitts’ law
978
979   We first verified (see Figure 9a) that the YKORM data is consistent with Fitts’ law, as in the previous studies. To do so,
980   we fit Fitts’ effective law using a mixed-effects linear model with fixed effects for ID𝑒 , strategy, and their interaction,
981
      and random intercepts for participants

         8     SYNTHESIS OF THE THREE STUDIES: COPULA FITS AND ID𝑒 MODEL
  1279
  1280   8.1    Copula fits
  1281
         Across the three studies, copulas consistently revealed a weak local dependence between ID𝑒 and MT. This suggests
  1282
  1283   that faster movements can also be more precise, and that this may actually be relatively common. Strategy introduces
  1284   somewhat stronger dependence. This suggests that when accuracy is enforced by W, movement are largely “equivalent”,
  1285
         whereas when accuracy is enforced by strategy, the participants seem to exerce more control, we believe e.g., by
  1286
         adjusting the strategy based on the previous trial.
  1287
  1288       In contrast, the global dependence across conditions is strong and robust, in line with the classical speed-accuracy
  1289   tradeoff. The first study favored the rotated Gumbel copula (mean 𝜃 ≃ 2), while the second and third studies favored
  1290
         Gaussian/t-copulas (average 𝜌 ≈ 0.8). Importantly, these copulas yield visually similar joint distributions (see Figure 18,
  1291
         where the top right panel and right panel of the third row are hardly dinstinguishable), so the discrepancy does not
  1292
  1293   undermine the main finding: ID𝑒 and MT are tightly coupled at the global level.
  1294       Could the difference between Gumbel and Gaussian fits be an artifact of model identifiability [26, 65]? To test this,
  1295
         we simulated datasets with realistic marginals and sample sizes comparable to our studies (Appendix F). The analysis
  1296
         shows that the rotated Gumbel should be identifiable given our data, ruling out a spurious explanation. We therefore
  1297
  1298   attribute the difference to the experimental protocols and input modalities: gesture input in study 1, physical arm
  1299   movements in study 2, and mouse pointing in study 3. We return to this interpretation in subsection 8.4.

1317
       Across all three studies, the EMG model consistently provided a better account of movement time than Gaussian
1318
1319   alternatives. More specifically, models with quadratic variance and asymmetry were strongly supported, highlighting

1324   Thus, we advocate for using the EMG model when modeling MT distributions.

1405   9 GENERATING POINTING DATA FOR DIFFERENT STRATEGIES: RATIONALE AND METHODS FOR THE
1406
             DIFFERENT MODELS
1407
1408   As Shmueli [58] argues, statistical models can serve two distinct purposes: to explain observed data or to predict new
1409   data. Success in one of these goals does not guarantee success in the other. In the preceding sections, our focus was
1410
       on explanation: we used models to probe theoretical questions about the effects of ID, ID𝑒 , strategy, and MT, and to
1411
1412
       understand the structure of pointing behavior. In this section, we shift to the predictive role. We ask whether the same
1413   models, when used generatively, can produce realistic pointing data, that can be used e.g., in simulations or in optimal
1414   design processes [51, 55].
1415
          We exploit the parametric models developed before (Gaussian bivariate model for (MT, ID𝑒 ), the EMG model for MT
1416
1417
       and the copula models) to generate synthetic data under different strategy conditions. A key feature of these models is
1418   their explicit parametrization of strategy, which allows us to simulate data for both pure-strategy and Fitts-with-strategy
1419   protocols. Note that we present only methods for the pure strategy protocol, but these methods generalize easily to
1420
       the Fitts-with-strategy protocol. As with any generative approach, there is a trade-off between simplicity and realism.
1421
1422
       Simpler models tend to be more robust and easier to generalize, but may miss “second-order” effects; here, we prefer
1423   simplicity.
1424      Throughout this section, Θ denotes a vector of model parameters, including strategy 𝑠. 𝑝 Θ (ID𝑒 , MT) refers to the
1425
       joint distribution between ID𝑒 and MT parametrized by Θ, and 𝑝 Θ (MT|ID𝑒 ) refers to the conditional distribution of MT
1426
1427
       given ID𝑒 . The three methods we present illustrate different ways to generate realistic synthetic pointing data, while
1428   highlighting trade-offs in flexibility, interpretability, and computational tractability. We use parameter values estimated
1429   from the three studies as concrete examples, giving researchers a realistic starting point; if the context differs, they can
1430
       easily re-estimate them on their own dataset.

    1653
    1654
            11.1      Models at a glance
    1655    Across this work, we introduced several models, which can complement or extend the traditional use of Fitts’ law.
    1656
    1657             • The probabilistic model for ID𝑒 in Equation 28 can be used to generate ID𝑒 values from nominal values of ID
    1658               and strategy.
    1659
                     • The bivariate Gaussian model, described in Equation 14 together with its parameters relationships to ID and
    1660
                       strategy described in Figure 10, allows generating ID𝑒 and MT values from nominal values. It provides a relatively
    1661
    1662               simple description, while still capturing effects of strategy and task geometry and part of the variability of
    1663               human movements.
    1664                                                                             32
       Pointing Beyond Fitts: Strategy, Movement Time Distributions, Local-Global Tradeoffs, and Generative Models
                                                                                                            CHI ’24, May 11–16, 2024, Honolulu, HI, USA


1665          • The three generative models described section 9, with their strengths and weaknesses previously discussed.
1666
1667
       Often, researchers can start with our reported parameters values if the goal is to predict movement times e.g., to design
1668   an intelligent system where movement times play a role. As we have used datasets acquired over a variety of conditions
1669   (pointing with a mouse, with the limb, or with gestures), we expecet our results to be quite robust. However, if the
1670
       context differs substantially, or to obtain more accurate predictions, these parameters can be re-estimated from new
1671
1672
       data using standard statistical methods.
1673
1674   11.2    On the local and global dependency
1675
       In this work, we examined both local and global dependencies between ID𝑒 and MT, corresponding to two levels of the
1676
1677   speed-accuracy tradeoff (SAT): a local SAT operating within conditions and a global SAT across conditions. Our finding
1678   of minimal to no local dependency suggests two possible explanations. One is that movement time variability due to
1679   internal processes such as internal motor noise [19, 62] dwarfs the variability induced by changes in ID𝑒 within a single
1680
       condition. Another is that movements executed under a fixed condition are functionally equivalent, leaving little room
1681
1682   for systematic tradeoffs to appear locally.
1683      To our knowledge, this local-global distinction has not been recognized in pointing models before. Yet, SAT effects
1684   operating at multiple scales have been examined in other domains, most notably reaction time research, where they
1685
       are referred to as micro and macro SATs [13, 16]. Interestingly, but perhaps only coïncidentally, the asymmetric,
1686
1687   long-tailed distributions we found to be critical for modeling movement times are also a hallmark of reaction times [44].
1688   A proposed explanation for the micro SAT is that individuals adjust their speed-accuracy tradeoffs while responding
1689   on a trial-by-trial basis [16], which suggests that the local tradeoff may have been obscured by large movement time
1690
       variability. Whether such mechanisms translate from tasks that are used to evaluate reaction times to motor control
1691
1692   remains an open question. What our results do show clearly is that the observed dependence between MT and ID𝑒 is
1693   much stronger at the global scale, across conditions.
1694
1695
       11.3    Applicability of copulas
1696
1697   We introduced copulas as a way to model the joint distribution of ID𝑒 and MT. To our knowledge, this is the first
1698   application of copulas in HCI. The main advantage of copulas is that they separate the dependency structure from
1699
       the marginals: one can represent the dependency with a compact parametric form (often just one or two parameters)
1700
1701
       without having to impose restrictive assumptions about the marginal distributions.
1702      Admittedly, the case we studied is not the most compelling for copulas; the dependency is already well captured by
1703   the EMG model, and our goal was not to position copulas as a central contribution of this paper, but rather to use it as a
1704
       tool to study dependencies with the least amount of assumptions. Nonetheless, we see strong potential for their use in
1705
1706
       HCI. Interaction, at its core, is about dependencies between variables20 , and in many situations these dependencies
1707   may be nonlinear or dominated by tail behavior (e.g., breakdowns). Copulas are designed for precisely such cases. We
1708   namely see promising opportunities in any setting where tail dependencies are expected i.e., where variable effects
1709
       become mutually reinforcing or diminishing at the extremes of their ranges.
1710
1711
          From a practical standpoint, copula tooling is still limited. The most mature implementations exist in R, and we could
1712   not find full-featured equivalents in Python. In many cases however, the most common copulas are widely available;
1713   many copula’s parameters can even be recovered from simple statistics without any dedicated software. For example,
1714
1715   20 Two variables that are independent can not describe a useful interaction scenario, since they can be understood one without the other.

1716                                                                             33

1717   for a 𝑡-copula, 𝜌 can be deduced from Kendall’s 𝜏 via 𝜌 = sin(𝜏𝜋/2), while 𝜈 can be obtained from tail dependence
1718
       coefficients using established formulas [12, Section 3].

       12    CONCLUSION
1721
1722   In this work, we presented models of pointing that serve two distinct but complementary roles. As explanatory tools,
1723
       they highlight the existence of two levels of the speed-accuracy tradeoff. As predictive tools, they allow realistic
1724
1725
       data generation. Together, these findings clarify the nature of the speed-accuracy tradeoff of pointing, and provide
1726   new models for researchers who wish to use them in simulation and optimisation contexts. For future research, we
1727   recommend exploring hierarchical pointing models as proposed in [73]. Notably, the GO dataset reveals considerable
1728
       inter-participant differences in ID𝑒 distributions across strategies (see supplementary materials): some participants
1729
1730
       display highly consistent distributions, indicating strong internal strategy adherence, while others perform faster
1731   conditions more precisely than accurate ones. Similarly, there are notable differences among participants in the
1732   variability of MT.

       A    PROTOCOLS FOR MANIPULATING THE SPEED ACCURACY TRADE-OFF
1867
1868   The SAT is well known in HCI: in most tasks, to achieve greater precision, users generally need to reduce their speed.
1869   However, reliably manipulating speed and accuracy conditions in controlled experiments is challenging; in pointing, at
1870
       least four distinct experimental protocols have been proposed.
1871
1872                                                                              36
       Pointing Beyond Fitts: Strategy, Movement Time Distributions, Local-Global Tradeoffs, and Generative Models
                                                                                                            CHI ’24, May 11–16, 2024, Honolulu, HI, USA


1873   A.1    Fitts’ protocol
1874
1875
       The most widely used protocol is Fitts’, where accuracy is dictated by the width of the target: the independent variables
1876   are target distance (D) and width (W), while movement time (MT) serves as the dependent variable. This paradigm
1877   closely aligns with meaningful HCI tasks, like icon selection, since target geometry is explicitly part of the task. However,
1878
       participants often fail to fully adhere to target width constraints, either overusing or underusing the available target
1879
1880
       space [36, 71].
1881
1882   A.2    Schmidt’s protocol
1883
       A second paradigm, due to Schmidt et al. [57], controls MT (speed) through a timing procedure, making MT and D
1884
1885
       the independent variables, and 𝜎 the dependent variable. This approach has mainly been applied to rapid movement
1886   studies, which are of lesser importance to HCI, and we do not consider this protocol further.
1887
1888   A.3    Pure strategy protocol
1889
1890   In the two aforementioned protocols, either speed or accuracy are considered independent variables, the other being the
1891   dependent one, but in reality both these variables are outcomes of human behavior. A third protocol, which we call the
1892   pure strategy protocol, thus uses D and strategic instructions (strategy) as independent variables, and MT (speed) and
1893
       𝜎 (accuracy) as the dependent variables [37]. Strategies are enforced by verbal instructions before each block, which
1894
1895   participants quickly learn to associate after some training. Note that there is no target width defined a priori in the pure
1896   strategy protocol.
1897
1898
       A.4    Fitts-with-strategy protocol
1899
1900   A fourth protocol [4, 46, 68] has the experimenter give strategic instructions to participants while they perform a
1901   classical Fitts task. Independent variables are strategy, D and W, while the dependent variables are MT and 𝜎. Empirical
1902
       results show that participants are in fact able to modify their strategies as in the pure strategy protocol, even in presence
1903
1904
       of a prescribed width, as reflected e.g., by changes in ID𝑒 for a given nominal ID level. For example in Mackenzie’s [46]
1905   experiment, on average participants erred up to 20% in the speed condition, but erred close to 0% in the accuracy
1906   condition. This protocol is the most complete one, since it accounts both for task properties (D and W) and user behavior
1907
       (strategy). Note that even when fitted on data from this protocol, and even adjusted with ID𝑒 , Fitts’ law does not link
1908
1909
       strategy, ID𝑒 and MT a priori since ID𝑒 can only be defined after the experiment has been conducted.

2017
       We considered copulas from the most widely recognized families: elliptical (e.g., the Gaussian and 𝑡 copulas), Archimedean
2018
2019
       (e.g., Clayton, Gumbel), and extreme value (e.g., HR, Galambos, 𝑡-EV), as well as their rotated variants21 and the
2020   independent copula. We utilized the R copula package [70] to fit these candidate copulas; copulas are estimated based
2021   on maximum likelihood estimation, and the marginals are estimated with empirical maximum likelihood estimation;
2022
       estimations are available by directly calling library functions, as illustrated in the code that comes with this paper.
2023

2025   21 Copulas can exhibit dependencies in the lower tails (low values) or upper tails (high values) of distributions. Given that pointing data in our dataset
2026   displays high variance at higher ID levels, copulas with upper tail dependence, such as the Gumbel copula, tend to perform poorly. By rotating the copula,
2027   we switch the dependence from the upper tail to the lower tail, providing additional copula candidates for consideration.

2029   C.2    Comparison procedure
2030
2031
       To compare copulas, we use the model evidence ratio R, which builds on AIC. A full exposition of model comparison
2032   based on AIC and R can be found in [3] but is summarized here for convenience. In the maximum likelihood estimation
2033   approach, the combination of model and parameter values that reaches the maximum log-likelihood L is preferred.
2034
       This leads to overfitting, as a nested model with fewer parameters can only do worse than the full model. One solution
2035
2036
       to that is AIC, a score that penalizes L with the number of parameters. A model’s AIC reads
2037
                                                            𝐴𝐼𝐶 = 2(𝑘 − L);                                                    (59)
2038
2039   a lower AIC indicates better fitting power. Models can then be compared based on their AIC difference, generally,
2040
       an AIC difference of 10 is considered very strong support i.e., the competing model is implausible. An interesting
2041
2042
       transformation of AIC differences is the relative likelihood ratio or model evidence ratio R
                                                                              
2043                                                              AIC1 − AIC2
                                                      R = exp −                                                                (60)
2044                                                                    2
2045
       which can directly be interpreted as odds (see also [63]). For example an 𝑅 = 0.125 means that model 2 is about 8 times
2046
2047   as likely as model 1, given current data.
2048       One difficulty is how to aggregate R from different datasets; for example, for different participants and replications.
2049
       Existing literatures lists three strategies:
2050
2051         • Pool the data before, then compute the loglikelihood L and R. This is justified when the data is considered
2052            homogeneous across the datasets.
2053
             • Compute the loglikelihoods L for each dataset, and sum them to get a single L. This is essentially equivalent
2054
                to pooling the data L, but we allow each dataset to be fitted by a model (copula) with different parameters. It is
2055
2056            thus best suited if one expects some heterogeneity between datasets.
2057         • Compute the loglikelihoods L for each dataset, and average them to get a single L. This is equivalent to the
2058
                previous method, except that when summing L, each dataset is “weighted” by its sample size, whereas when
2059
                averaging L each dataset is equally weighted.
2060
2061   Since we expected differences between experimental conditions, we did not pool the data. We considered the sum of
2062   the likelihoods rather than the averages to deal with unsuccessful fits. Indeed, an unsuccessful fit results in a NaN (not
2063
       a number). When averaging, NaNs are not taken into account, but they are when summed (in which case their value is
2064
2065   0). Therefore, averaging likelihoods does not penalize fits that did not succeed.

       Linear Mixed-effect Models (LMMs) are useful when dealing with hierarchical or clustered data, such as repeated
2136   measurements on the same participants as in this work[39].22 The advantage of using LMMs over traditional linear
2137   models in an experiment with repeated measurements is that they better estimate the parameters variances, thereby
2138
       reducing type-1 and type-2 errors and avoiding the problem of “pseudo-replication” [39].
2139
2140       Scaling values. The typical values of D and W is given in pixel and can reach values beyond 1000, but regression
2141
       coefficients are more easy to interpret when the dependent variables have similar scales. So, we scale D by 1/1000 and
2142
2143   W by 1/100 for each model. In the JGP dataset, we computed that 40px = 1cm, while for the YROMK dataset, 37px = 1cm,
2144   and for the PDD dataset, 36px = 1cm. Hence, the three scales are almost equivalent. However, considering we have no
2145   information about the transfer function used in the JGP and the YROKM datasets, we do not know if these measures are
2146
       truely comparable. Strategy is converted to the numerical scale [-1, 0.5, 0, 0.5, 1], and treated as a continuous variable.

           Model selection. For model selection, we follow Zuur et al. [74], who suggest selecting the best model based on

2150
       comparing likelihoods obtained with maximum likelihood (ML) estimation on the classical linear model, but report the
2151   parameters of the LMM obtained with restricted maximum likelihood estimation (MERL). Hence, the tables feature the
2152   parameter estimated obtained via MERL and the likelihoods obtained with ML. Fitting with ML was more prone to

       failures to converge, but cycling through different optimization methods always resolved this.

2156
2157   One hypothesis to explain the discrepency between the best fitting copulas for the global dependency is that the
2158
       rotated Gumbel could be difficult to identify: perhaps we sometimes observe the Gaussian copula “by default’. We thus
2159
2160
       performed a simulation to identify how easily the Gaussian and rotated Gumbel copulas can be discriminated. We
2161   considered two cases
2162
             • first, we generated a joint distribution using the rotated Gumbel copula with typical marginals from the JGP
2163
2164
                dataset. We then identified the rotated Gumbel and Gaussian copulas on this generated dataset, and computed
2165            the model evidence ratio R. This was performed for 100 repetitions for increasing sample sizes. This condition
2166            is labeled continuous in Figure 19.
2167
             • second, we wanted to evaluate the effect of blocking on identifiability (see [26] to see how a choice of stimulus
2168
2169
                level can modify the identifiability of computational models). To do so, we would have had to sample ID𝑒 levels,
2170            and then sample from the conditional form of the rotated Gumbel copula. Unfortunately, at the time of writing,
2171            the conditional form of rotated copulas was not available in the library that we used. Therefore, we performed
2172
                a bootstrap analysis instead, where we select a random dataset out of the 15 participants × 4 replications, from
2173
2174
                which we sampled data with replacement. Then, the procedure is identical to the first case; the resulting R is
2175            labeled blocks in Figure 19.
2176
           Figure 19 displays log R, which implies that values below 0 indicate support for the rotated Gumbel copula. This
2177
       analysis shows that with the size of the datasets used (240 movements per dataset), and the replications (4 per participant),
2178
2179   the rotated Gumbel copula should be properly identifiable. Hence, the observed difference between the two experiments
